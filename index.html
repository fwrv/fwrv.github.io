(15 Dec 2015) First step was to find a domain name. Growing up, my family used to rent a cabin in Chocorua, New Hampshire for a couple of weeks every summer. Mt. Converting this site's content into JS objects and eventually JSON files

(Talking the talk on 12 Feb 2016, walking the walk soon) The "great leap forward" for this site will be when content can be displayed / modified in a modular and scalable way. Right now, the site you are looking at is one long html file. (If you're using a chrome browser, you can right-click/control-click on the screen, hit "Inspect", and see the code for yourself.) Each update that I make, I type into the same file. This is increasingly untenable as the number of updates grows - most importantly, it's inelegant!

As a key part of my javascript education, I've been learing about objects. Within the context of js, an object is a "complex" data type, as opposed to numbers or strings of text, which are "simple" data types. Objects are useful in that they can take on multiple properties. For example, a smartphone-contact object could contain the properties of a person's name, their number and their email address. Curating an array (or list) of objects all with similar (or overlapping) properties allows you to list or search within quite easily (using "for" loops, among other nifty tricks).

The next step, then, for this site/project is to convert the content posts into objects, each with a set of properties. The common properties as I've laid them out include:

  • Title
  • Date posted
  • Tag (i.e. whether it counts as "making", "reading", or "eating")
  • Summary (a one sentence description which can be displayed in an index or TOC)
  • Author (for a future scenario in which the BLC may contain kind outside contributors)
  • Content (i.e. the post itself)

When the content lives in the form of js objects, it will free the index.html page (and other html sub-pages, as I make them) from housing all the content in the same doc. You, the viewer, will be able to search and sort content by tag, date or author or even browse by summary. The entire content process will become more modular and I'll be able to abstract fundamental app changes without getting ensnared by a long thorny html file. It is the future and it is exciting.

However, this can be improved upon still further! You may notice a shortcoming of the plan so far: By turning content posts into js objects in a single array I may be taking them out of the html file, but I'm only just moving them all into a js one. This is where JSON comes in. JSON, or "JavaScript Object Notation" is a format and file type that will allow me to create content posts as independant files with the same data parameters that I would feed into the objects in my standard js file. I haven't fully gotten my head around this yet - but the idea is that each of the content posts would live as its own file in my github repository and my main BLscript.js file would reference them as described above.

These changes will bring about a brave new world for the members of the Big Lake Club. I'll be providing unsolicited updates as I go along.

(12 Feb 2016): "Business Models, Information Technology, and the Company of the Future" by Stanford's Haim Mendelson showed up this morning in the daily newsletter from the MIT tech review (the piece was originally published in Oct 2015). The heady title gives a sense of what to expect from the piece, which is long on MBA-lingo and short on real-world examples / applications. (Seriously though, this is an actual quote: "The combined effect of mobile technologies, wearable devices and sensors, cloud computing, and big data technologies will sharpen the structure of future business models." Say what? No way!) That being said (and as pejorative as I made "MBA-lingo" sound just now), I found the following interesting:

Three technologies will play a particularly important role in the use of IT:

  • Customer-intimacy agents will be customers digital representatives in the marketplace, using data to find and solicit solutions that will make them better off.
  • Value-chain coordinators will match supply and demand, assemble customized solutions, and engage in electronic customer data-driven innovation.
  • Producers will increasingly engage in marketplace selling and data-driven innovation.

These three technologies seem to fit along a continuum of being customer facing vs. enterprise facing. "Customer-intimacy agents" purport to be actively champion-ing (even anticipating) the needs/desires of the customer/user. An example of this could be (my employer) Knewton's adaptive learning technology, which uses student proficiency data to cater content to the individual learner's needs and goals (and showing teachers predictive analytics re: student performance).

"Value-chain coordinators" straddle the best interests of the customer and the enterprise, in theory connecting the one with the best possible services of the other. An obvious example of this would be how Uber uses surge pricing to match ride supply with ride demand. Surge pricing itself is unsustainably punitive and really a manifestation of equilibrium breaking down rather than a triumph of efficiency - it's more of a stop-gap. The victory for "value-chain coordinating" technology will be when Uber can anticipate ride demand so well so that surge pricing wont even take place. Maybe you could debate that Uber would never want its own technology to make surge pricing obsolete but I think users will ultimately be powerful enough that it (Uber) won't have a choice (the power of Uber's users is also why European regulators and cab drivers are fighting a losing battle). This anticipatory tech would be far easier to implement when cars go driver-less. (For another example: see how Amazon is stocking it's "fulfillment centers" similarly to anticipate customer/user demand.

Mendelson's last category is also probably the most boring as it's already the most prevalant. Producers are selling into an increasingly flat market (see the post on ubiquitous/accessible content above) where intimate knowledge of their end users (and use cases) is imperative both for differentiation and innovation.

For all the drivel (Mendelson's and mine), I think there is actually good food for thought here. (26 Feb 2016): Chris Dixon's awesome "What's Next in Computing" opens with an obvious but astute point: the "tech industry", including investors, entrepreneurs and the media are highly focused on where we are in the financial cycle, while often losing sight of where we are in the product cycle. He then takes the reader on a quick tour of the last three product cycles or "eras":

  • The PC Era: Which gave us desktop / workplace / productivity apps
  • The Internet Era: Which allowed for search engines, e-commerce / saas and social networks
  • The Mobile Era: Which have revolutionised on-demand services and is estimated to provide connectivity to c. 4.5 billion people in the next several years

The products and companies we associate with these eras are probably easy to recite: Microsoft and Apple for the first, Google, Amazon, Salesforce and Facebook for the second and Apple (again), Uber, WhatsApp and Instagram (aka FB again) for the third. (N.B. Obviously not an exhaustive list.) He goes on to say that each of these cycles have two phases:

  1. a gestation phase, when the platform/technology is cumbersome, prohibitively priced or poorly implemented and
  2. the growth phase, when new products come along that solves these problems.

We are clearly in the growth phase of the Mobile and Internet Eras (and maybe in the twilight of the PC one). The article gets really exciting when it starts to look at what possible Era might come next, i.e. where we're in the gestation phase. Dixon highlights these three:

  • Artificial Intelligence
  • Internet of Things
  • Virtual Reality

While I'm not sure (and neither is Dixon) whether any of these will represent a new "era" but they could certainly be revolutionary based on how their adoption plays out (He also talks about drones and cars, which have so much going on I've set aside for now. Also shout-out to quantum computing.). What's exciting to me about all three of these arenas is that it feels like we're already seeing the tip of the iceberg. The "revolution" hasn't taken place in the sense that IoT/VR products (in particular) are still niche toys and AI is mostly making news for beating humans at very nerdy Chinese board games. That said, there is clearly momentum building. A couple of names have started to emerge (DeepMind, Nest, Oculus), allowing people to make fairly educated guesses as to who the big players will be - and these players are very well funded by very powerful people/companies (Alphabet is willing to burn $3.6bn a year on its "big bets"). Any "start-up" in one of these three spaces needs to be ready to differentiate themself. On the positive side, if they can produce something special, BigTech has deep pockets for inorganic growth too. Several promotional efforts also suggest that this technology will be reaching the masses in 2016 in a way it hasn't before. Samsung is offering free Gear VR headsets with the purchase of Galaxy S7 edges while IBM is offering new API's for its Watson technology seemingly every month. In any case, by the end of the year it's likely we will have moved beyond the gestation phase for at least one of these new technologies.

London Burger Power Rankings

(29 Feb 2016) In the spirit of the late great Grantland, I thought the best way to keep tabs on London's burger scene would be through maintaining some Power Rankings. Power Rankings are wonderful in that they are wholly subjective and yet can be as rigorous / analytical as desired. They can cover topics of any genre, from College Basketball teams to Mad Men episodes to Liam Neeson's "Action Dad" roles (Note: All of these examples are Grantland examples as Grantland was / will be the most creative and insightful source of Power Ranking in the history of the art form). Without further ado, my burger power rankings:

  1. Patty & Bun (Jose Jose burger): Patty & Bun is at that sweet spot: Juicy, tasty toppings with a real, thick, meaty patty. The other items on the list don't quite match up with P&B here (MeatMission is saucy but the beef is not special, Bar Boulud does have nice meat but pretty much everything else is a shambles - more on this below). A Brioche bun guarantees that meat, chorizo and semi-spicy JJ sauce flavors are enjoyed to the fullest.
  2. Bukowski (X-Tudo, Brixton location): I'm not sure why this doesn't make it onto more best-London-burger lists. Bukowki burgers have big, bad patties - to the point that I probably wouldn't have a double (though I might have 2 burgers to enjoy a second menu item). The Brixton market location is probably the most fun setting of the ones on this list. Really all of the menu items bring something to the table (mostly spilled condiments), but the X-Tudo takes the top thanks to its multiple cheeses, chilli aioli and avocados (making it healthy). The Smokey Beast is great, although maybe a bit gratuitous.
  3. MeatMission (Dead Hippy burger): Another conventional choice, but this is a delicious burger that usually takes me all of 100 seconds to finish. It represents the pinnacle of the the "greasy goodness" burger-type. Dead Hippy sauce and cheese are everywhere. The patties are relatively thin so you don't get the red-in-the-middle texture of a girthier version, but there are two of them which makes up for it and they have this special "chuck steak", which they assure me signifies quality. MeatMission also offers the Monkey Fingers, which I inhale.
  4. Honest Burger (the Louisiana burger): Honest makes a great burger, not a superlative one, but a great one. The patty is thick enough, the bread is never stale and the french fries are always on point. The special Louisiana Burger boasted a Cajun sauce, thin crispy onion rings and pickles. The eponymous Honest burger off their standard menu is good too.
  5. Burger & Lobster (Burger): It's annoying that this burger costs GBP 20, it's also annoying that I still get it because I like it that much. Great patty, great bread, no special spicy sauce or anything keep this from being a top-burger contender. Goes well with lobster roll.

In the democratic spirit of the internet, I don't let a lack of burger expertise get in the way of me having and sharing opinions. PLEASE weigh in with yours. I'm eager to take part in any burger debate, particularly if resolving the debate involves eating burgers.

A note on Bar Boulud: I know a lot of people think these are wonderful. I really don't (and I've tried them multiple times). The bread gets in the way - I wouldn't say it's stale but it's stiff enough that it's distracting the rest of the meal. It (the bread) also gets super-flakey and ends up falling off all over the plate (or the nice shirt that I'm probably wearing if I'm at Bar Boulud). I'm not saying I'm the cleanest eater in the world, but I certainly don't have the same bread problem with any of the names on this list. While this list is meant to be price agnostic - I would say the price doesn't help my impression.
